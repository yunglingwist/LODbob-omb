Documentation

This project was developed for the Information Science and Cultural Heritage course within the University of Bologna's Master's degree program in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge. Its core purpose is to semantically link various cultural and media objects related to the Scott Pilgrim universe on the web using RDF, with the overarching goal of contributing to the preservation and enhanced accessibility of modern cultural heritage.

Scott-Pilgrim-LOD: Linked Open Data for Multimedia Cultural Heritage

As our team shares a deep fascination with contemporary transmedia storytelling and the intersection of different pop-culture mediums, we were naturally drawn to selecting a theme that bridges graphic novels, cinema, and music. Among several candidates, the Scott Pilgrim universe stood out for its unique narrative structure and its profound impact on global youth culture. The Scott Pilgrim franchise is far more than a simple story about a protagonist and his relationships. It embodies a complex ecosystem of transmedia adaptations, where the original Canadian graphic novels, the cult-classic film directed by Edgar Wright, the retro-style video game, and the recent anime production interweave. This interconnectedness makes it an ideal subject for semantic modeling and Linked Open Data, where meaningful connections between diverse entities—such as fictional characters, the real-world actors who portray them, and the musical inspirations that define their world—can be expressed and visualized. Additionally, because the Scott Pilgrim universe has been extensively documented in both fan-driven wikis and official international databases like Wikidata, it allows for the integration of existing open data resources. By mapping these resources using RDF, we can explore how a single creative work evolves across different platforms. This project supports both the preservation and the reinterpretation of modern digital heritage through semantic tools, highlighting the cultural significance of 21st-century multimedia narratives.

Item Selections: Mapping a Multi-Centered Ecosystem

The item selection process was not designed as a linear extraction from a single source, but rather as an exploration of a multimedia ecosystem. While the Scott Pilgrim narrative is widely known, we realized that its cultural value lies in the "dialogue" between different formats. Therefore, our selection reflects a non-centralized approach, where we curated items that represent the intersections of comics, cinema, music, and real-world geography. During our brainstorming sessions, we moved beyond the plot-driven elements to include "peripheral" yet semantically rich entities. We were drawn to the indie rock heritage of the 90s, Canadian architectural landmarks, and the material culture of musical gear. This led to a diverse collection where a real-world guitar (like the Rickenbacker 4003) or a real band (like Plumtree) holds as much semantic weight as a fictional character. These selections allowed us to build a Knowledge Graph that bridges the gap between fictional lore and real-world cultural heritage. To identify and validate these items, we conducted research across institutional and open data collections such as Wikidata, DBpedia, and MusicBrainz. We used ChatGPT to assist in cross-referencing metadata across these platforms, helping us identify objects that already possessed stable identifiers (URIs). This was crucial for ensuring that our digital collection was not just a list of items, but a set of nodes ready for Linked Open Data (LOD) integration. Our final collection was managed through a structured collaborative environment, prioritizing items that offered the highest relational potential. For example, selecting Casa Loma as a landmark allowed us to link the fictional "Evil Ex" movie set back to a tangible historical site in Toronto. This methodology ensured that our project captures the transmedia fluidity of the theme, where information flows freely between the textual descriptions in the scripts, the visual representation in the anime, and the material reality of the objects themselves.

Conclusions

This project demonstrates that transforming heterogeneous media data into RDF is more than a technical task; it is an exercise in knowledge construction. By explicitly modeling the relationships between a fictional narrative and its real-world cultural context, we have shifted from isolated data points to a rich semantic network that supports discovery, reuse, and long-term digital preservation.


The evolution of the web from a document-centric system to a Web of Data—the Semantic Web—highlights the importance of graph structures. These models allow machines to interpret the "meaning" and context of information. By applying these principles to the Scott Pilgrim universe, we have shown how contemporary popular culture can be curated with the same scholarly rigor as traditional cultural heritage objects.


Major platforms and knowledge engines now rely on these graph-based paradigms to enhance content understanding and discoverability. In the LAM (Libraries, Archives, Museums) environment, structuring data as linked graphs makes cultural narratives meaningfully discoverable by hem humans and algorithms, ensuring that the dynamic network of transmedia meanings continues to evolve.

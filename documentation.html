Documentation

This project was developed for the Information Science and Cultural Heritage course within the University of Bologna's Master's degree program in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge. Its core purpose is to semantically link various cultural and media objects related to the Scott Pilgrim universe on the web using RDF, with the overarching goal of contributing to the preservation and enhanced accessibility of modern cultural heritage.

Scott-Pilgrim-LOD: Linked Open Data for Multimedia Cultural Heritage

AOur team has a real passion for modern transmedia stories and how different pop culture mediums come together. After considering several options, we chose the Scott Pilgrim universe for its unique storytelling and huge impact on global youth culture. Scott Pilgrim is much more than just a story about a guy and his relationships. It's a rich ecosystem of interconnected media—the original Canadian graphic novels, Edgar Wright's cult classic film, the retro video game, and the recent anime series all weave together. This makes it perfect for semantic modeling and Linked Open Data, where we can express and visualize connections between fictional characters, their real-world actors, and the music that defines their world. Plus, Scott Pilgrim is well-documented across fan wikis and international databases like Wikidata. Using RDF to map these resources lets us explore how one creative work evolves across different platforms. This project preserves and reinterprets modern digital heritage through semantic technologies, showcasing the cultural importance of 21st-century multimedia stories.

Item Selections: Mapping a Multi-Centered Ecosystem

We didn't just pull items from one source in a straight line. Instead, we explored the entire multimedia ecosystem around Scott Pilgrim. While everyone knows the main story, we saw its real cultural value in how different formats "talk" to each other. So we picked items that show the intersections between comics, movies, music, and even real geography. During brainstorming, we went beyond the main plot to include "side" elements that were semantically rich. We got excited about 90s indie rock heritage, Canadian landmarks like Casa Loma, and actual musical gear. This gave us a diverse collection where a real guitar (Rickenbacker 4003) or real band (Plumtree) has equal importance to fictional characters. Our Knowledge Graph bridges fictional stories with real cultural heritage. To find and verify these items, we dug through Wikidata, DBpedia, and MusicBrainz. ChatGPT helped cross-reference metadata across platforms, identifying items with stable URIs—essential for Linked Open Data integration. We managed our collection collaboratively, prioritizing items with the most connections. For example, choosing Casa Loma let us link the fictional "Evil Ex" movie set to a real Toronto historical site. This approach captures Scott Pilgrim's transmedia nature, where info flows between scripts, anime visuals, and real-world objects.

Conclusions

This project demonstrates that transforming heterogeneous media data into RDF is more than a technical task; it is an exercise in knowledge construction. By explicitly modeling the relationships between a fictional narrative and its real-world cultural context, we have shifted from isolated data points to a rich semantic network that supports discovery, reuse, and long-term digital preservation.


The evolution of the web from a document-centric system to a Web of Data—the Semantic Web—highlights the importance of graph structures. These models allow machines to interpret the "meaning" and context of information. By applying these principles to the Scott Pilgrim universe, we have shown how contemporary popular culture can be curated with the same scholarly rigor as traditional cultural heritage objects.


Major platforms and knowledge engines now rely on these graph-based paradigms to enhance content understanding and discoverability. In the LAM (Libraries, Archives, Museums) environment, structuring data as linked graphs makes cultural narratives meaningfully discoverable by hem humans and algorithms, ensuring that the dynamic network of transmedia meanings continues to evolve.
